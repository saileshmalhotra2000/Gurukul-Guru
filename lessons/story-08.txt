Wednesday, 4 March 2026
The eastern sky blushes softly. Dew rests on each blade of grass like silent pearls. Rama and Lakshmana sit before Vashishtha, the hush of dawn wrapping the forest in stillness.
Rama speaks first.
“Gurudev… if ego rests on Dharma instead of praise, what protects it when insult comes?”
A heron lifts from the river, its wings cutting through the pale mist.
Vashishtha watches it rise.
“When wind strikes a mountain, does the mountain argue?”
Lakshmana smiles faintly. “No, Gurudev.”
“Why?”
“Because it stands on firm ground.”
Vashishtha turns to Rama.
“When ego stands on praise, it stands on moving wind.”
He draws a line in the damp soil.
“When ego stands on Dharma, it stands on earth.”
Rama lowers his gaze.
“And how does it learn to stand there?”
Vashishtha reaches for the clay pot and places it upright between them.
“If this pot is empty, even a small push will topple it.”
He fills it halfway with water.
“And now?”
Lakshmana presses it gently. It steadies.
“What gives it weight?” Vashishtha asks.
“The water,” Rama answers.
“Yes.”
He looks at them both.
“The water is inner clarity.”
Silence spreads like light through the trees.
“When you know why you act… insult has little hold.”
Lakshmana’s brow tightens.
“But the sting is felt.”
“Yes,” Vashishtha says calmly. “The body feels heat. The heart feels tightening.”
He places his palm on his chest.
“But spacious ego watches the sting without becoming it.”
Rama’s breath deepens.
“How?”
Vashishtha points to the river once more.
“When a stone is thrown into the river, ripples arise.”
They watch the current.
“Does the river run away from the ripple?”
“No.”
“Does it cling to it?”
“No.”
“It allows the movement… and returns to flow.”
The first rays of sun touch Rama’s face.
“When insult comes,” Vashishtha continues softly,
 “ask within — ‘Is it true?’”
Rama nods slightly.
“If true, let it refine you.”
“And if it's untrue?” Lakshmana asks.
“Let it pass like an echo fading in the hills.”
A faint breeze stirs the treetops.
“Ego becomes steady,” Vashishtha says,
 “when it no longer rushes to defend every shadow.”
He picks up a fallen leaf.
“This leaf does not protest the wind.”
He releases it. It drifts quietly.
Rama speaks in a low voice.
“So steadiness comes from knowing oneself.”
Vashishtha’s eyes soften.
“Yes.”
“When you know your intention is pure,
 you need not fight every sound.”
The forest awakens — birds calling, branches whispering.
“Remember,” Vashishtha says gently,
 “the servant who knows his duty does not abandon his post because someone mocks him.”
Lakshmana straightens instinctively.
“And the king within?” Rama asks quietly.
Vashishtha smiles.
“The king within is untouched.”
The river flows on, indifferent to praise, unmoved by insult —
 full, steady, ancient.
And in the stillness of that dawn,
 the servant learns to root itself in earth,
 while the sky within remains vast.
