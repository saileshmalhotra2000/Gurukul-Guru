Stage 3 → Stage 4: The Earliest Departure of Attention
May 28, 2026
The evening wind moved softly through the sal trees.
The gurukul had become quiet after the day’s training.
Some students cleaned the stone pathways.
 Some washed their wooden bowls beside the river.
 A few sat silently beneath the banyan tree, watching dusk gather slowly through the forest.
Rama and Lakshmana sat before Vashishtha.
No one spoke for a long time.
Only the sound of insects.
 The distant river.
 The occasional crack of bamboo in the cooling night.
Then Vashishtha picked up a dry leaf from the ground.
He held it above his palm.
And released it.
The leaf drifted slightly eastward before touching the earth.
Vashishtha asked quietly:
“Rama…
 when did the wind move the leaf?”
Rama replied:
“The movement became visible only after the leaf had already begun drifting, Gurudev.”
Vashishtha nodded.
“Yes.
And long before the leaf moved,
 the wind had already arrived.”
Silence.
Then he continued:
“The mind is the same.
Reaction is only the visible leaf.
But the movement began earlier.”
Lakshmana asked:
“Earlier than emotion itself?”
“Yes,” said Vashishtha.
“Much earlier.
Before irritation,
 there is leaning.
Before grasping,
 there is movement.
Before distraction,
 there is a tiny abandoning of presence.”
Rama listened carefully.
Vashishtha drew a line slowly in the sand.
“When attention leaves the present,
 it does not leap suddenly.
It tilts.”
He tilted his hand gently to one side.
“Only slightly.
Almost invisibly.”
He looked toward the river.
“Watch carefully in ordinary life.
A man is speaking to you.
Outwardly you still face him.
But inwardly,
 attention has already begun traveling elsewhere.
Toward agreement.
 Toward defense.
 Toward anticipation.
 Toward memory.
The body remains.
The mind has already departed.”
Lakshmana lowered his eyes.
As if recognizing something directly.
Vashishtha continued:
“Most men notice only the completed reaction.
Very few notice the departure.”
He touched his chest lightly.
“The departure is subtle.
Like a thread being pulled from cloth.
Like a horse turning its head before running.
Like water beginning to slope before becoming a stream.”
Rama asked softly:
“Gurudev…
 why is this first movement so difficult to see?”
Vashishtha smiled faintly.
“Because the movement promises something.”
Silence.
“Becoming.
 Protection.
 Completion.
 Relief.
 Importance.
Attention becomes fascinated by where it is going.
So it forgets to observe the going itself.”
A bird suddenly flew from the branches above.
Lakshmana’s eyes lifted immediately toward the sound.
Vashishtha noticed.
“There,” he said gently.
“The body moved naturally.
Nothing was wrong.
But observe carefully:
after the sound,
 did attention fully return?”
Lakshmana became still.
After a few moments he said:
“No, Gurudev.
Part of attention remained with the disturbance.”
“Yes.”
Vashishtha nodded.
“This is how fragmentation begins.
Not through great events.
Through tiny unfinished pullings.”
The forest grew darker.
The students in the distance had become silhouettes against firelight.
Vashishtha spoke again:
“When attention is repeatedly pulled outward without awareness,
 the mind begins living in fragments.
One fragment speaks.
 Another anticipates.
 Another replays.
 Another resists.
Then presence weakens.
And psychological time strengthens.”
Rama asked:
“Psychological time?”
Vashishtha drew three points in the sand.
“One point remembers.
 One point imagines.
 One point fears.
And attention travels endlessly between them.”
He erased the center point.
“But rarely rests here.”
Long silence followed.
Only the sound of the river remained.
Then Vashishtha said quietly:
“Rama…
the deepest chains are not the reactions themselves.
The deepest chains are the unnoticed movements toward them.”
Ordinary Life Examples
While speaking with someone
The other person is still talking,
 but inwardly:
you begin preparing your reply
defending yourself
seeking approval
waiting to impress
resisting discomfort
The leaning has already begun.
While checking a message
Before opening the device,
 there is often:
a subtle pull
an incompleteness
a reaching outward
The hand moves after the leaning.
During irritation
The tightening in the chest is not the beginning.
Earlier there was:
a small resistance
a refusal
a movement away from “what is”
During distraction
Attention rarely vanishes suddenly.
First comes:
slight restlessness
micro-avoidance
a search for stimulation
movement away from simple presence
Today’s Sadhana
Tonight and tomorrow,
 do not focus on stopping reactions.
Instead:
Observe departures.
Very gently notice:
when attention begins leaning away
when anticipation begins
when inner acceleration starts
when the mind arrives before the body
when presence becomes secondary to psychological movement
Do not correct it immediately.
First learn to see it clearly.
Especially observe:
 the first half-second.
That is where unconsciousness begins gathering momentum.
Before leaving, Rama asked:
“Gurudev…
 what happens when this first movement is seen completely?”
Vashishtha looked into the dark forest.
Then replied softly:
“When the first movement is fully seen…
many reactions end
 before they are born.”
