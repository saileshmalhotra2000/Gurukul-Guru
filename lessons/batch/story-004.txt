The forest is awake earlier today.
Mist still clings to the grass. A koel calls once, then falls silent, as if remembering where it is.
Rama and Lakshmana return to the same place by the riverbank. The sand still bears the faint marks of yesterday’s circles, half-smoothed by wind.
Vashishta is already there.
He is watching the water.
Neither boy speaks. They sit.
Time passes.
At last, without turning, the Rishi says,
 “Rama, what followed you after we parted yesterday?”
Rama answers slowly.
 “Awareness, Gurudev.”
Vashishta nods.
 “And what followed the awareness?”
“A subtle pride,” Rama admits. “That I had noticed it.”
Lakshmana glances at him, surprised.
Vashishta smiles—not kindly, not sharply.
“Even the watcher wishes to be admired,” he says.
 “This is good to see.”
He turns now, his gaze steady.
“Lakshmana.”
“Yes, Gurudev.”
“Yesterday, when others praised Rama, what moved in you?”
Lakshmana straightens.
 “Joy for my brother.”
“Only joy?”
Lakshmana hesitates.
 “…and a wish that I too be seen.”
Vashishta draws a long line in the sand with a twig.
“This,” he says, “is desire that announces itself.”
Then he draws a second line, finer, almost invisible.
“And this is desire that hides behind virtue.”
He looks at Lakshmana.
“Which is more difficult to uproot?”
“The hidden one,” Lakshmana answers quietly.
Vashishta closes his eyes briefly.
“Good. You are learning to smell smoke before fire.”
A leaf falls into the river. It spins once, then is carried away.
Vashishta points to it.
“Rama. Does the river resist the leaf?”
“No, Gurudev.”
“Does it chase it?”
“No.”
“Yet the river moves it.”
He turns back to them.
“Character is not stiffness.
 It is direction.”
Rama listens intently.
“When praise comes, move forward.
 When blame comes, move forward.
 Do not stop to argue with either.”
Lakshmana frowns.
 “But Gurudev, how do we know if we are moving forward or merely drifting?”
Vashishta’s eyes sharpen.
“Excellent question.”
He rises slowly and walks a few steps away, then stops.
“Stand,” he says.
Both boys stand.
“Close your eyes.”
They obey.
“Now,” Vashishta says softly, “imagine the world has forgotten you.
 No name.
 No lineage.
 No praise.
 No blame.”
The forest sounds grow louder in their absence.
“Tell me,” he continues, “what remains?”
Rama breathes steadily.
 “A sense of rightness, Gurudev. A pull toward what must be done.”
Lakshmana swallows.
 “A restlessness… but beneath it, the same pull.”
Vashishta opens his eyes.
“That pull,” he says, “is your north.”
He taps the ground with his staff.
“Character is staying aligned to that pull even when the world pulls harder.”
He gestures for them to sit again.
“Today’s discipline,” Vashishta says, “is simple.”
He looks first at Rama.
“Observe not only your reactions—
 observe your satisfaction with restraint.”
Then to Lakshmana.
“Observe not only your loyalty—
 observe where it seeks reward.”
Neither boy speaks.
The river continues its teaching without words.
After a long silence, Vashishta adds, almost as if to himself:
“Kings fall not because they lack virtue,
 but because they grow fond of their own reflection.”
The sun is higher now.
The lesson does not end with dismissal.
 It ends with watchfulness.
And the forest receives them back into its quiet test.
Can you please elaborate the following “Observe not only your reactions— observe your satisfaction with restraint.”
The river is quieter today.
Rama and Lakshmana sit as before. The sunlight now reaches the water, scattering gold across its surface.
Rama looks toward Vashishta.
“Gurudev… yesterday you said, ‘Observe not only your reactions—observe your satisfaction with restraint.’ I do not fully understand.”
Vashishta studies him for a long moment.
Then he picks up a small stone and tosses it lightly into the river.
Ripples spread outward.
“Rama, when Lakshmana insulted you, what arose first?”
“A tightening,” Rama says. “A wish to respond.”
“And when you did not respond?”
“There was quiet.”
“And after the quiet?”
Rama pauses.
“…a small satisfaction.”
Lakshmana turns toward him.
“Satisfaction?”
Rama nods slowly.
 “Yes. As though I had passed a test.”
Vashishta’s eyes sharpen.
“There.”
He draws a circle in the sand.
“First comes impulse.”
He draws a second circle around it.
“Then comes restraint.”
He draws a third, faint circle.
“And then comes pride in restraint.”
Lakshmana frowns.
 “Is it wrong to feel pleased for doing right?”
Vashishta shakes his head gently.
“Not wrong. But dangerous.”
He leans forward slightly.
“If you begin to love the image of yourself as disciplined…
 you will begin performing discipline.”
Rama listens very carefully now.
Vashishta continues:
“When a man restrains anger and then thinks,
 ‘See how calm I am,’
 the ego has simply changed clothing.”
Lakshmana’s eyes widen slightly.
“So even silence can feed pride?”
“Yes,” Vashishta replies quietly.
 “Especially silence.”
The river moves steadily beside them.
Vashishta picks up a dry leaf and places it in Rama’s palm.
“If you throw this leaf into the water and it floats,
 does the river congratulate itself?”
Rama shakes his head.
“It simply flows.”
“Exactly.”
He looks directly into Rama’s eyes.
“Restraint must become as natural as flowing.
 When you notice yourself enjoying the feeling of being superior for your restraint—
 observe that too.”
Rama lowers his gaze.
“I did feel that, Gurudev.”
“Good,” Vashishta says calmly. “Then you are awake.”
He straightens.
“The first victory is over reaction.
 The second victory is over the pride of victory.”
Lakshmana exhales softly.
“And if we notice pride?”
Vashishta smiles faintly.
“Do not fight it.
 Do not condemn it.
 Do not feed it.”
He gestures toward the river.
“Let it pass, as you let anger pass.”
A breeze moves through the trees.
After a long silence, Vashishta adds:
“The ego does not disappear in one stroke.
 It refines itself.
 You must refine your seeing.”
Rama bows slightly.
Lakshmana’s posture softens.
The forest holds the teaching without echo.
And the watching continues. 🌿

The river is brighter today. Sunlight rests on its surface like a thin sheet of gold.
Rama sits quietly, but his eyes are alert.
“Gurudev,” he says softly, “you said the ego changes clothing. What clothing does it wear?”
Vashishta does not answer immediately.
He lifts his upper cloth from his shoulder and lays it on the ground.
“Rama, what is this?”
“A garment, Gurudev.”
“And if I place it over a stone?”
He drapes it over a small rock.
“What does it appear to be?”
Lakshmana leans forward.
 “A form… almost like a seated figure.”
Vashishta nods.
“The stone has not changed. Only the covering.”
He looks at Rama.
“Anger says, ‘I am stronger than you.’
 Restraint says, ‘I am better than you.’”
Lakshmana blinks.
Vashishta continues calmly:
“When anger shouts, the ego wears armor.
 When restraint whispers, the ego may wear robes.”
He lifts the cloth again.
“In anger, it says, ‘How dare you insult me?’
 In false calm, it says, ‘Look how superior I am for not reacting.’”
Rama’s breath deepens slightly.
Vashishta watches him.
“The first is obvious.
 The second is refined.”
Lakshmana speaks carefully.
“So ego can hide inside virtue?”
Vashishta’s eyes soften.
“Yes.”
He gestures toward the river.
“A man may give charity and think,
 ‘I am generous.’
 He may forgive and think,
 ‘I am nobler than others.’
 He may remain silent and think,
 ‘I am more disciplined.’”
He pauses.
“The clothing changes. The ‘I’ remains inflated.”
Rama lowers his gaze.
“Gurudev… when I felt pleased with my restraint,
 was that ego wearing new cloth?”
“Yes,” Vashishta replies gently.
 “But do not be disturbed.”
He places his hand lightly over his own heart.
“The ego is not your enemy.
 It is your instrument—if seen clearly.”
Lakshmana asks,
 “How do we know when it has changed clothes?”
Vashishta smiles faintly.
“When there is comparison.”
Silence falls.
“If your calm depends on feeling above another,
 ego is dressed as virtue.”
The wind moves through the trees.
Vashishta concludes softly:
“True restraint leaves no self-image behind.
 It is like the river flowing—
 without announcing, without measuring, without remembering.”
Rama absorbs this in stillness.
Lakshmana’s fire has grown quieter.
And the watching deepens again. 🌿

The morning is softer today.
A thin veil of cloud filters the sun. The river moves in long, quiet strokes.
Rama has been silent since the last teaching. Lakshmana watches him, curious.
At last, Rama speaks.
“Gurudev… yesterday you said, ‘The ego is not your enemy. It is your instrument—if seen clearly.’”
He lifts his eyes.
“How can that which binds us also serve us?”
Vashishta studies him with interest.
He reaches beside him and picks up a bow resting near Lakshmana.
“Lakshmana, what is this?”
“A bow, Gurudev.”
“Is it dangerous?”
“Yes.”
“Should we destroy it?”
Lakshmana hesitates. “No… it is useful.”
Vashishta nods.
“If the bow controls the archer, there is danger.
 If the archer controls the bow, there is protection.”
He places the bow across his knees.
“The ego is like this bow.”
Rama listens closely.
Vashishta continues:
“Without ego, you would not stand upright.
 You would not say ‘I must learn.’
 You would not protect, build, speak, or act.”
He looks directly at Rama.
“When you say, ‘I must be worthy,’
 who speaks?”
Rama reflects. “The sense of ‘I,’ Gurudev.”
“Yes.”
He taps his chest lightly.
“That sense of ‘I’ is necessary. It gathers your strength. It gives direction.”
Lakshmana interjects,
 “Then why does it cause suffering?”
Vashishta smiles faintly.
“Because the bow begins to imagine it is the archer.”
He lets the words settle.
“When ego forgets it is a tool and declares,
 ‘I am the center of all things,’
 it binds.”
He draws a small circle in the sand.
“This is healthy ego:
 ‘I am responsible.’
 ‘I will act.’
 ‘I will stand firm.’”
Then he draws a larger circle over it.
“This is distorted ego:
 ‘I must win.’
 ‘I must be admired.’
 ‘I must never be seen as small.’”
Rama speaks softly.
“So ego is not to be destroyed… but understood.”
Vashishta’s eyes warm.
“Exactly.”
He gestures toward the river.
“See that current? It gives movement to the water. Without it, the river stagnates.”
He pauses.
“But if the current becomes violent and forgets the banks, it floods.”
Lakshmana leans forward.
“How do we see ego clearly?”
Vashishta answers quietly.
“Notice when ‘I’ becomes heavy.”
Rama tilts his head slightly.
“Heavy?”
“Yes.
When you act and feel light—ego is serving.
 When you act and feel tight—ego is ruling.”
He looks at Rama carefully.
“When you restrained anger and felt a small satisfaction, that was natural.”
He pauses.
“When that satisfaction became identity—
 ‘I am calm, I am superior’—
 the instrument began to command.”
Silence flows between them.
After a time, Vashishta concludes:
“Do not fight the ego.
 Do not worship it.
 Hold it.”
He places the bow back down gently.
“As a warrior holds his weapon—
 with awareness.”
The river continues its quiet teaching.
And the boys sit, learning not to destroy their strength—
 but to master it. 🌿
