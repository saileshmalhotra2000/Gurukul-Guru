Before dawn had fully broken, Vashishtha called Rama and Lakshmana to the riverside.
A thin mist floated above the water.
The world was neither night… nor day.
“Stand here,” he said.
They stood beside him, watching the faint outline of the opposite bank.
After some time, Lakshmana spoke softly,
“It is difficult to see clearly.”
Vashishtha nodded.
“Yes.”
“Because light is neither absent… nor complete.”
Rama observed carefully.
Shapes appeared… then disappeared.
Trees seemed near… then far.
Nothing was certain.
Vashishtha asked:
“In this light… can you trust your eyes fully?”
Lakshmana replied,
“No, Gurudev.”
“They show… but not clearly.”
Vashishtha then said:
“Walk forward… slowly.”
They stepped ahead.
The ground felt different underfoot.
Each step required attention.
What looked smooth was uneven.
What seemed distant was close.
After a short distance, Vashishtha called them back.
They returned.
He then spoke:
“This is how the mind sees when it is clouded.”
“Not darkness…”
“…but half-light.”
Rama listened deeply.
“Not ignorance…”
“…but incomplete understanding.”
Vashishtha’s gaze rested on him.
“Yes.”
The eastern sky began to brighten.
Light slowly spread across the river.
Forms became clearer.
Edges sharpened.
Distance made sense again.
Lakshmana said,
“Now everything is easy to see.”
Vashishtha asked,
“What changed?”
Rama answered,
“Nothing outside…”
“…only the light increased.”
Vashishtha nodded.
“Truth does not change.”
“Perception does.”
The sun’s first rays touched the water.
The mist began to dissolve.
What was confusing moments ago… became obvious.
Vashishtha concluded:
“Do not rush to judge in half-light.”
“When the mind is unclear…”
“…even the right path appears wrong.”
“Wait…”
“Let understanding become complete.”
The brothers stood silently.
The lesson was simple… yet profound.
Not all confusion comes from darkness.
Much of it…
comes from seeing too soon.
And as the sun rose fully…
the world revealed itself without effort.

Is waiting enough for the mind to become clear
Ah… this is a very honest question. 🌿
 Even Lakshmana would not remain silent here.
You ask:
“Is waiting enough?”
Vashishtha would look at you for a moment… and then say:
“No.”
And then, after a pause:
“But restless doing is also not the way.”
Come, understand this carefully.
What is “waiting”?
If waiting means:
sitting in confusion
letting the mind keep spinning
hoping clarity will come on its own
Then no…
That waiting is like mud being constantly stirred.
It will not settle.
What actually brings clarity
Clarity comes when two things happen together:
1. You stop disturbing the water
2. You allow it to settle
Both are needed.
1. Stop disturbing the mind
Notice what usually happens:
a thought comes
you react to it
you add more thoughts
you argue, imagine, justify
This is like throwing more pebbles into the pond.
So first step:
Do not add more disturbance
Just see the thought.
Let it be.
2. Allow natural settling
Now… when you are not interfering…
something begins to happen on its own.
Like muddy water left untouched.
Slowly…
heaviness settles
clarity rises
This is not created by you.
It is revealed.
So what is the right attitude?
Not “doing”
 Not “waiting passively”
But something in between:
Quiet, alert allowing
How Vashishtha would guide Rama
If Rama was confused, he would not say:
“Just wait.”
He would say:
Sit
Be still
Watch
Do not react immediately
And then…
“Let the mind show you clearly.”
A simple way to practice this
When confusion comes:
Pause
Do not decide immediately
Watch what the mind is doing
Do not add more thinking
Stay with it quietly
After some time…
you will notice:
some thoughts fade
some clarity appears
what felt urgent becomes simple
The deeper truth
Clarity is not something you create.
It is what remains when confusion is not fed.
One final line Vashishtha might say
“Do not rush to act in muddy water…
 and do not keep stirring it while you wait.”
So no…
waiting alone is not enough.
But quiet, aware non-interference…
that is enough.
