The sun has now risen above the eastern trees. Golden light spreads across the riverbank where Vashishtha, Rama, and Lakshmana remain seated.
The four marks Vashishtha drew in the sand are still visible.
Truth. Responsibility. Respect for life. Restraint.
A gentle wind brushes across them, but the shapes remain.
Rama studies them quietly.
“Gurudev,” he says, “if these laws protect life, why do people still break them?”
Lakshmana nods immediately.
“Yes. Everyone knows lying creates trouble. Yet people still lie.”
Vashishtha smiles slightly.
“Come.”
He stands and walks toward a nearby mango tree.
At its base lies a fallen fruit, half-eaten by birds.
Vashishtha picks it up and holds it out.
“Rama, why do birds peck this fruit?”
“Because it is sweet.”
“And if the fruit is overripe?”
Lakshmana answers quickly.
“They still eat it.”
“Even if it harms them?”
Lakshmana hesitates.
“Yes.”
Vashishtha places the fruit back on the ground.
“Desire,” he says softly, “often blinds wisdom.”
They walk slowly along the riverbank.
“Most people know the laws of Dharma,” he continues.
“But knowing is not the same as mastering oneself.”
Rama listens carefully.
“The mind,” Vashishtha says, “is easily pulled by pleasure, fear, anger, and pride.”
Lakshmana sighs.
“So even when a person knows the right path…”
“…the mind drags him toward the easier one,” Vashishtha finishes.
They stop where the river narrows between two rocks.
The water rushes faster here.
Vashishtha points.
“See the current.”
Rama nods.
“It pulls strongly.”
“If you drop a leaf into this water, can it resist the current?”
“No.”
“But what if the leaf becomes a boat?”
Lakshmana’s eyes brighten.
“Then it can cross the river.”
Vashishtha nods.
“Discipline turns the drifting leaf into a boat.”
The river continues its restless movement.
“Truthfulness,” Vashishtha says, “requires courage.”
“Responsibility requires effort.”
“Respect for life requires compassion.”
“Restraint requires mastery over desire.”
He looks at both brothers.
“These are not easy.”
The wind stirs the tall grass along the bank.
“That is why societies repeat these laws generation after generation.”
“So that each child hears them again.”
Rama gazes across the flowing water.
“And practices them.”
“Yes.”
Lakshmana watches a fish leap briefly from the surface.
“So Dharma is simple to understand… but difficult to live.”
Vashishtha laughs softly.
“Very well said.”
He begins walking back toward the shade of the trees.
“Remember this, Rama.”
The brothers follow him.
“Dharma does not fail because it is weak.”
He pauses and looks back at them.
“It fails when people grow lazy in protecting it.”
The river flows endlessly beside the forest.
And the four marks remain quietly in the sand.
