The forest was still dim when Rama and Lakshmana arrived at the eastern edge of the gurukul grounds.
Morning had not fully awakened yet. The sky held a soft grey-blue light, and the birds had only just begun their first calls.
Vashishtha stood near a tall banyan tree, looking toward the horizon where the sun would soon rise.
The princes bowed and stood beside him.
For a while the three of them watched the quiet sky.
Then Vashishtha spoke.
“Today we will learn something subtle.”
Lakshmana straightened with interest.
The sage pointed toward the horizon.
“Soon the sun will appear. Watch carefully.”
They waited.
Slowly, the first edge of the sun rose above the distant trees, casting a soft golden glow across the land.
Light spread over the river, over the grass, and through the forest leaves.
Vashishtha asked quietly,
“Did the sun choose where to shine?”
Rama shook his head.
“No, Gurudev.”
“The light simply spreads.”
Vashishtha nodded.
“Attention can be like this.”
Lakshmana looked thoughtful.
The sage continued.
“Most people use attention like a narrow beam.”
He pointed to a small patch of sunlight on the ground between them.
“They force it onto one thing, then another, chasing objects again and again.”
Lakshmana recognized this immediately from his own experience.
“But there is another way.”
Vashishtha slowly gestured to the entire forest around them.
“Let attention open like sunlight.”
“Wide.”
“Relaxed.”
“Not clinging to any single thing.”
The princes listened carefully.
“Stand quietly,” Vashishtha instructed.
“Let your attention include everything you can notice.”
“Do not focus on one sound or one sight.”
“Simply allow the whole morning to be present.”
They stood silently.
Lakshmana first noticed the breeze moving through the banyan leaves.
Then the distant sound of the river.
Then the warmth of sunlight slowly touching his face.
Normally his attention would jump quickly from one thing to another.
But now it rested more broadly, holding many things at once.
Rama felt the same openness—sounds, light, breath, and stillness all appearing within a quiet field of awareness.
After some minutes Vashishtha spoke again.
“What did you notice?”
Lakshmana answered slowly.
“My mind became calmer.”
Rama added,
“Nothing needed to be chased.”
Vashishtha smiled gently.
“Yes.”
“When attention is wide, the mind settles naturally.”
He looked at the rising sun now fully above the horizon.
“Wisdom does not come from forcing the mind into stillness.”
“It comes from understanding how attention works.”
The forest was now bright with morning light.
Students could be seen moving along distant paths beginning their daily studies.
Vashishtha turned toward the gurukul.
“Come.”
As they walked back, he said quietly,
“Tomorrow we will explore something even more surprising…”
“…the place from which attention itself arises.”
Rama and Lakshmana followed him beneath the banyan branches, their awareness moving calmly with each step.

At first it looks like a contradiction:
Sometimes the instruction is narrow attention (breath, candle, sound).
Sometimes the instruction is wide attention (open awareness of everything).
But these are two different trainings of the same instrument.
Think of your tabla practice.
Some days you practice one bol slowly — Dha… Dha… Dha… — refining the stroke.
Other times you play the entire theka flowing through the tala, feeling the whole rhythmic field.
Both are necessary.
Attention training works the same way.
1. Narrow attention (the breath practice)
This develops stability.
When you rest attention on the breath:
the mind stops jumping everywhere
attention learns to stay
distractions become visible
You are training steadiness.
Without this step, attention becomes weak and scattered.
This is why many traditions begin here.
2. Wide attention (open awareness)
Once attention has some stability, we relax it.
Instead of holding one object, we allow awareness to include:
sounds
sensations
breath
space
thoughts
Now attention becomes spacious and relaxed.
You are training clarity without tension.
This prevents concentration from becoming tight or forced.
3. Why both are necessary
If you only train narrow attention:
the mind can become rigid
concentration becomes effortful
If you only train wide attention:
the mind becomes vague and dreamy
attention lacks strength
So the ancient teachers trained both muscles.

 Vashishtha did not usually explain things with philosophy. He showed them through direct experience.
Here is a small teaching he could have given Rama and Lakshmana.
The Forest Experiment
One morning Vashishtha brought the princes to a clearing in the forest.
He said nothing at first.
Birds were calling from many directions. The river could be heard faintly through the trees.
After a while he said to Rama:
“Listen carefully to the river.”
Rama closed his eyes and listened.
After a few breaths he said,
 “Yes, Gurudev, I hear it.”
Then Vashishtha said:
“Now listen to the birds in the trees.”
Rama shifted his attention.
“Yes, Gurudev.”
Then the sage said:
“Now notice your breathing.”
Rama nodded again.
Lakshmana was watching carefully.
After some time Vashishtha asked a question.
“Rama, what moved?”
Rama replied,
“My attention moved, Gurudev.”
The sage nodded.
Then he asked another question.
“Did the river move inside you?”
Rama smiled slightly.
“No.”
“Did the birds move inside you?”
“No.”
“Did the breath move inside you?”
“No.”
Then Vashishtha asked the important question.
“That which knew the river…
 that which knew the birds…
 that which knew the breath…”
He paused.
The forest was very quiet.
Then he asked softly:
“Did that move?”
Rama sat still.
For the first time he looked directly into his own experience.
The attention had moved many times.
But something else had remained exactly where it was.
After a long silence Rama said quietly:
“No, Gurudev.”
Vashishtha smiled.
“That which does not move…
 yet knows all movement…”
He pointed gently to Rama’s chest.
“Know that well.”
What this experiment reveals
You can try this yourself right now.
Move your attention between:
a sound
your breath
a sensation in the body
the room around you
You will see:
Attention moves easily.
But the knowing of it does not move.
That silent knowing is what the sages called:
awareness
the witness
the seer
Not something mystical.
Just something so constant that we normally overlook it.
This was the beginning of the training Vashishtha gave Rama — not to control the mind, but to recognize the one who sees the mind.
And once a student begins to see that clearly…
the entire path of self-knowledge opens.
