The forest wakes slowly.
Mist lingers between the sal trees, and the river carries the faint gold of early light. Birds call once, then again, as if testing the morning.
Rama and Lakshmana sit where they did yesterday. Neither speaks at first.
Vashishtha breaks a twig between his fingers.
“Yesterday,” he says, “we spoke of praise.”
Lakshmana’s spine straightens slightly.
“And of the ego that hides when it is hunted.”
He looks at Rama.
“Tell me, Rama — what did you notice after we rose from here?”
Rama answers after a pause.
“When we walked back, villagers bowed. I felt warmth arise… and then the wish to appear unaffected.”
Vashishtha nods.
“And which was heavier?”
Rama reflects.
“The wish to appear unaffected.”
A faint smile touches the Rishi’s face.
“That weight,” he says, “is the mark of hidden struggle.”
Lakshmana listens intently.
Vashishtha continues.
“When the ego is attacked, it does not vanish.
It learns manners.”
He picks up a smooth stone from the riverbank.
“A crude ego boasts: ‘I am great.’
A refined ego whispers: ‘I am beyond greatness.’”
He tosses the stone into the water. It disappears without sound.
“Both sink you,” he says, “if unseen.”
Lakshmana speaks carefully.
“Then what is right conduct, Gurudev?”
Vashishtha does not answer immediately. Instead, he watches the river.
“Lakshmana,” he says at last, “when hunger comes, do you insult it?”
“No.”
“Do you glorify it?”
“No.”
“What do you do?”
“I eat… when it is time.”
Vashishtha turns.
“So with praise.
So with blame.
So with ego.”
Rama’s eyes remain steady.
“Neither feast nor fast unnecessarily.”
“Yes,” Vashishtha says.
A breeze moves through the leaves.
“The error,” he continues, “is not feeling pleasure.
The error is building a house there.”
Lakshmana exhales slowly.
“So even subtle pride must be seen?”
“Especially subtle pride.”
Vashishtha’s voice grows quiet.
“The loud ego can be corrected by the world.
The silent ego survives in saints.”
The words settle heavily.
Rama bows his head slightly.
“Then vigilance is endless?”
Vashishtha shakes his head.
“No. Vigilance matures into ease.”
He looks toward the sky, where the mist is thinning.
“At first, you must watch.
Later, seeing happens by itself.”
Lakshmana’s brow smooths.
“And until then?”
“Until then,” Vashishtha says gently,
“do not make enemies inside your own house.”
The forest brightens as the sun clears the trees.
“Let thoughts come.
Let feelings come.
Let praise sing and insults shout.”
He looks at both of them.
“You are not the host of a court.
You are the ground beneath it.”
The river continues its quiet speech,
indifferent to who listens.
