Dawn came quietly to the forest.
A pale mist rested over the ground, and the first rays of sunlight touched the tops of the tall sal trees around the gurukul. The students were beginning their morning chores—some sweeping the pathways, others bringing water from the river.
Rama and Lakshmana finished their duties and walked toward the clearing where Vashishtha often taught.
The sage was already there.
He sat beside a small fire, feeding it thin twigs. The flames moved gently, rising and falling with the morning breeze.
The princes bowed and sat before him.
Vashishtha looked at the fire for a while before speaking.
“Yesterday you saw a moment of silence in the mind.”
Both princes nodded.
Lakshmana said, “It appeared only briefly, Gurudev.”
“Yes,” Vashishtha replied calmly. “That is its nature at first.”
He pointed toward the fire.
“Tell me, Lakshmana—what makes this fire burn?”
“The wood, Gurudev.”
“And if I stop adding wood?”
Lakshmana watched the flames thoughtfully.
“Then the fire will slowly fade.”
Vashishtha nodded.
“The mind is very similar.”
He drew a small line in the dust with a twig.
“Thoughts continue because we keep feeding them.”
Lakshmana asked, “With what do we feed them?”
Vashishtha looked up at the rising smoke.
“With attention.”
Rama listened carefully.
The sage continued.
“When attention clings to every thought, the mind grows restless.”
“But when attention is no longer given to each passing thought, the mind begins to quiet—just like a fire without new wood.”
At that moment a squirrel darted across the clearing, scattering dry leaves.
Lakshmana’s attention followed it immediately.
Vashishtha noticed and smiled.
“Did you see that?”
Lakshmana nodded.
“My attention moved again.”
“Good,” Vashishtha said.
“To see it move is already the beginning of mastery.”
He then gave them a small practice.
“For the next few minutes, sit and watch the breath.”
“Do not control it.”
“Simply observe it as it enters and leaves.”
“If a thought comes, do not fight it.”
“Just do not feed it.”
The princes closed their eyes.
The forest slowly awakened around them—birds calling, leaves rustling, the distant sound of water flowing.
Lakshmana noticed a thought appear.
I wonder how long we will sit.
For a moment his attention followed it.
Then he remembered the instruction and let it pass.
Rama’s breathing became steady and quiet.
Minutes passed gently.
Finally Vashishtha spoke.
“Open your eyes.”
They did.
The sage looked pleased.
“Today you learned something important.”
He pointed again to the small fire beside him, now burning more softly.
“You do not need to fight the mind.”
“You only need to stop feeding every thought that appears.”
The smoke rose slowly into the brightening sky.
Vashishtha stood and brushed the dust from his robe.
“Tomorrow,” he said as they began walking back through the forest path,
“we will see how attention can be guided—not by force, but by understanding.”
Rama and Lakshmana followed him through the morning light, each step quiet and thoughtful.
