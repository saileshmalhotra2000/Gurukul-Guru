The river bends gently through the forest where Vashishtha has led the two princes. The morning sun now rests higher among the branches, and the mist has almost vanished.
The three stand silently for a while.
The water glides past them without pause.
Vashishtha does not speak.
Rama watches the river carefully, remembering the instruction.
“Notice who is watching.”
Lakshmana shifts slightly from one foot to the other, trying to remain patient.
At last he whispers to his brother.
“What do you see?”
Rama answers quietly.
“I see the water moving… the leaves floating… the sunlight on the surface.”
Lakshmana nods.
“Yes, I see those too.”
Vashishtha, still gazing at the river, speaks calmly.
“And who sees them?”
Lakshmana immediately replies.
“We do, Gurudev.”
The sage turns his eyes toward him.
“You see the river?”
“Yes.”
“You see the trees?”
“Yes.”
“You see your brother?”
“Yes.”
Vashishtha steps closer.
“Can you see the one who is seeing?”
Lakshmana opens his mouth to answer, but no words come.
He looks puzzled.
Rama’s brow tightens slightly as he turns inward.
Vashishtha kneels and places his hand on the surface of the water. The current slips past his fingers.
“The eyes see the world,” he says.
“The mind understands what is seen.”
“But behind both… there is the witness.”
Rama listens with deep stillness.
Lakshmana scratches his head slightly.
“The witness?” he asks.
“Yes.”
The sage picks up a small stone and drops it into the river.
The surface ripples outward in widening circles.
“Did you see the ripples?”
“Yes,” both brothers reply.
“Did the witness ripple?”
Lakshmana looks confused again.
Rama slowly shakes his head.
“No.”
Vashishtha nods.
“The thoughts in the mind are like ripples.”
“The emotions are like currents.”
“The senses are like waves touching the shore.”
The river continues its endless motion.
“But the witness,” he says softly, “remains unmoved.”
Lakshmana stares at the flowing water.
“So even when the mind becomes restless…”
“…the witness is still,” Rama completes quietly.
Vashishtha smiles faintly.
“Yes.”
A breeze moves through the forest canopy.
Leaves flutter down and drift onto the water.
“Most people,” the sage continues, “live their entire lives inside the ripples.”
“They believe they are the waves.”
Lakshmana watches a leaf spinning slowly in the current.
“And the one who discovers the witness?”
Vashishtha looks toward the far bank where the sunlight touches the grass.
“He steps onto the shore.”
The brothers fall silent.
The river keeps moving, but something inside the stillness of the forest feels deeper now.
Vashishtha rises.
“Come.”
They begin walking back toward the ashram through the tall trees.
After several steps, Rama speaks softly.
“Gurudev…”
“Yes, Rama.”
“When I paused this morning… I may have glimpsed it.”
The sage’s eyes shine with quiet approval.
“Good.”
Lakshmana quickly asks,
“Can I see it too?”
Vashishtha chuckles gently.
“You already are.”
Lakshmana stops walking.
“What?”
The sage continues walking ahead, his voice drifting back through the trees.
“The witness you seek… is the one asking the question.”
The forest path grows quiet again.
And the river flows on, carrying leaves, branches, and sunlight — while the unseen witness remains perfectly still.

The three of them have returned to the clearing near the river. The morning sun is now warm on the ground, and the sound of water moving over stones fills the quiet space between them.
Rama has been silent for some time.
At last he speaks.
“Gurudev… you said, ‘the one who discovers the witness steps onto the shore.’”
Vashishtha nods slightly.
Rama continues.
“But who is the one discovering the witness?”
Lakshmana looks up quickly.
“Yes… if the witness is the one who sees everything… who discovers it?”
Vashishtha’s eyes soften.
“Good,” he says.
He picks up a clay bowl and fills it with water from the river.
Holding it steady, he asks Rama,
“What do you see?”
“My reflection, Gurudev.”
“And when you first looked into the bowl… did you know your reflection was there?”
“No.”
“So when you recognized it… who discovered it?”
Rama pauses.
“I did.”
Vashishtha tilts his head slightly.
“And who is that ‘I’?”
Rama closes his eyes briefly.
Lakshmana watches with growing curiosity.
“The mind?” Lakshmana suggests.
Vashishtha shakes his head gently.
“The mind is also seen.”
He taps the side of the bowl.
“When a thought arises, do you know it?”
“Yes.”
“Then the thought is not you.”
Lakshmana frowns.
“What about feelings?”
“When anger arises, do you know it?”
“Yes.”
“Then anger is also known.”
The sage slowly pours the water back into the river.
“The discovery of the witness,” he says softly, “is not made by another thing.”
Rama listens with deep stillness.
“It is the moment when attention turns back upon itself.”
Lakshmana blinks.
“Like… when the eye tries to see itself?”
Vashishtha smiles.
“Yes.”
“You cannot see the eye directly.”
“But suddenly you understand that all seeing was happening through it.”
Rama’s breath becomes very quiet.
“So the ‘one discovering’ is only a moment of recognition.”
Vashishtha nods.
“Yes.”
“For a moment the mind becomes still enough…”
He points gently toward Rama’s chest.
“…and the witness recognizes itself.”
Lakshmana tilts his head.
“So nothing new is created?”
“No.”
“It was always present.”
The river glides past them, shining in the sunlight.
Vashishtha concludes quietly.
“The discovery of the witness is like waking from a dream.”
“In the dream you searched everywhere.”
“But when you wake…”
Rama finishes softly.
“…you realize you were already the one who was awake.”
The forest hums with life around them.
And the river continues to flow —
seen by eyes,
known by mind,
yet resting quietly in the awareness that never moves.
