The first light of dawn filtered gently through the tall sal trees of the forest. Mist still rested over the ground like a thin veil. In the quiet courtyard of the gurukul, the birds had begun their morning songs.
Rama and Lakshmana had already finished their ablutions and stood waiting beside the small fire altar. The soft smoke of the sacred fire rose into the cool air.
Sage Vashishtha emerged from his hut, walking slowly with his staff. His eyes were calm and clear, as if they carried the stillness of the forest itself.
“Gurudeva,” Rama and Lakshmana said together, bowing respectfully.
Vashishtha looked at them with gentle approval. “You have risen before the sun again. This is good. A student who greets the dawn learns from the world before the world becomes noisy.”
He gestured for them to sit beneath a large banyan tree.
“Today,” he said, “we will speak about attention.”
Lakshmana leaned forward slightly. Rama remained still, listening.
Vashishtha picked up a small leaf from the ground and held it in his hand.
“Look at this leaf carefully,” he said.
The princes looked at it. It was simple—green with a thin line of veins running through it.
“What do you see?” the sage asked.
“A leaf, Gurudeva,” Lakshmana replied.
Vashishtha smiled faintly. “Yes… but that is only the first glance.”
He turned the leaf slowly.
“In this leaf is the work of the sun, the patience of the tree, the nourishment of the soil, the gift of the rain, and the passage of many days.”
Rama’s eyes softened with understanding.
“When the mind is quiet,” Vashishtha continued, “nothing in the world is small. Every object becomes a teacher.”
He then placed the leaf on the ground between them.
“From today onward, I want each of you to practice seeing fully. When you look at something—whether a leaf, a river, or a person—do not look quickly. Look with care.”
Lakshmana asked, “Gurudeva, how will this help us?”
Vashishtha replied, “A prince who sees clearly will rule wisely. A warrior who sees clearly will act only when needed. And a seeker who sees clearly begins to understand the truth behind appearances.”
The sun slowly rose higher, golden light spreading through the forest.
“Come,” Vashishtha said gently, standing. “Today we will walk to the river. The river has many lessons for those who truly observe.”
Rama and Lakshmana rose and followed their teacher along the narrow forest path, their minds quiet and attentive.
The day’s learning had just begun.
The Two-Minute Forest Practice
1. Sit quietly
Sit comfortably.
Let the body be relaxed but upright — as if you are sitting under a tree listening to the forest.
Do nothing for a few breaths.
Just arrive.
2. Let the senses open
Do not search for anything.
Simply notice what is already present.
Perhaps:
distant sounds
the movement of air
light in the room
sensations in the body
Let everything appear naturally.
Nothing needs to be pushed away.
3. Notice attention moving
Soon you will see something interesting.
Attention moves.
It may go to:
a sound
a thought
an itch
a memory
a plan for later
Do not stop it.
Simply notice:
“Attention has moved.”
This is exactly what Rama began to notice beside the river.
4. Now notice the deeper thing
Each time attention moves, ask very gently:
“What noticed that?”
Do not answer with words.
Just feel into the question.
For a brief moment something very quiet becomes noticeable — the silent observer that was present before the thought, during the thought, and after the thought.
Stay there for a moment.
5. Let everything continue
Thoughts will return.
Sounds will come.
Attention will wander again.
Each time, simply notice:
movement
noticing
quiet
Nothing needs to be controlled.
Just watch.
