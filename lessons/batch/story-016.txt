The forest is quiet in the early dawn.
Mist drifts slowly above the river, and the first rays of the sun touch the tops of the tall sal trees. Rama and Lakshmana sit on smooth stones near the water, waiting.
Vashishtha arrives silently along the narrow path.
The brothers rise and bow.
“Sit,” the sage says gently.
They return to their places.
For a time, no one speaks. The sound of the flowing river fills the space between them.
At last Vashishtha points toward the water.
“Rama, what do you see?”
“The river, Gurudev.”
“And what is it doing?”
“It is flowing.”
Lakshmana smiles faintly.
“It never stops.”
Vashishtha nods.
“Look more carefully.”
The brothers watch the current.
A fallen branch rests partly in the water. The flow breaks around it, forming swirling eddies.
“Lakshmana,” Vashishtha says, “what happens when the river meets that branch?”
“The water circles around it.”
“And does the river become angry with the branch?”
Lakshmana chuckles.
“No, Gurudev.”
“Does it argue with the branch?”
“No.”
Rama’s gaze remains on the water.
“The river simply continues.”
Vashishtha nods slowly.
“Yes.”
He picks up a small twig and tosses it into the current.
The twig spins for a moment in the eddy before being carried downstream.
“Observe carefully,” the sage says.
“The river does not fight obstacles.”
“It moves around them.”
Lakshmana tilts his head.
“Is the mind supposed to be like that, Gurudev?”
Vashishtha smiles faintly.
“The mind tries to be like that.”
Rama looks up.
“But it is not.”
“No.”
The sage draws a line in the damp sand beside them.
“When someone insults a man, what does the mind do?”
Lakshmana answers quickly.
“It stops.”
Vashishtha nods.
“Yes.”
“The mind clings to the branch.”
He draws small circles in the sand.
“It turns around the insult again and again.”
Rama speaks softly.
“Like the eddy in the river.”
“Yes.”
Lakshmana watches the water closely.
“But Gurudev… the river eventually carries the twig away.”
Vashishtha looks at him with approval.
“Well seen.”
“The river does not hold the twig.”
“The current remains stronger than the obstacle.”
The wind rustles the leaves overhead.
“But the human mind,” the sage continues, “often forgets its current.”
Rama asks quietly,
“What is the current of the mind?”
Vashishtha points toward the rising sun.
“Awareness.”
Lakshmana looks puzzled.
“So awareness keeps the mind flowing?”
“Yes.”
“When awareness is strong, thoughts pass like twigs in the current.”
“And when awareness is weak…”
Lakshmana finishes slowly,
“…the mind circles the obstacle.”
The sage nods.
“Anger, insult, pride, fear.”
“These become branches in the river.”
Rama gazes at the flowing water for a long moment.
“So the problem is not the branch.”
“No.”
“The problem is forgetting the current.”
The sun now rises fully above the forest, turning the river into a ribbon of gold.
Lakshmana watches a leaf drifting downstream.
It spins briefly in a small whirlpool — then the current catches it again.
He smiles slightly.
“The river remembers its direction.”
Vashishtha’s eyes shine gently.
“Yes.”
“And when the mind remembers awareness…”
“…it continues its journey.”
The three sit silently as the river moves past them, steady and untroubled, carrying leaves, twigs, and reflections of the morning sky.

“Gurudev… you said awareness becomes obvious when the mind is clear.”
Vashishtha nods.
“But obvious to who?”
Lakshmana looks up immediately.
“Yes… if awareness is the witness… who recognizes it?”
Vashishtha bends and draws a small circle in the sand.
“Rama, when you dream at night, who sees the dream?”
“The mind, Gurudev.”
“And when you wake up, what happens to the dream?”
“It disappears.”
“And who knows that it disappeared?”
Rama pauses.
“I do.”
Vashishtha looks at him carefully.
“That ‘I’ which knows the dream has ended…”
He wipes the circle away.
“…is not the dream.”
Lakshmana leans forward.
“So awareness knows the mind?”
“Yes.”
“But when the mind becomes quiet,” Lakshmana says, “who notices awareness?”
Vashishtha smiles faintly.
“Imagine a man searching for the lamp that lights his house.”
Lakshmana laughs softly.
“But he is already holding the lamp.”
“Yes.”
“The moment he stops searching outside…”
He lifts his hand slowly.
“…the light is already there.”
Rama’s breath becomes very slow.
“So awareness does not become obvious to another thing.”
“No.”
“It becomes obvious to itself.”
Lakshmana frowns slightly.
“How can something know itself?”
Vashishtha points toward the sun rising above the trees.
“Does the sun need another lamp to illuminate it?”
“No.”
“The sun shines by its own light.”
He looks back at them.
“In the same way, awareness reveals everything.”
“When the mind becomes quiet…”
“…nothing else stands between awareness and itself.”
The river flows steadily beside them.
Rama lowers his gaze slightly.
“So the question ‘to who?’ disappears.”
Vashishtha nods.
“Yes.”
Lakshmana stares at the flowing water for a long time.
