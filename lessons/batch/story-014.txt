The forest had grown deeper.
The familiar sounds of the ashram were now far behind them. The path had narrowed into a thin thread between tall sal trees whose trunks rose like pillars into the pale morning sky. Sunlight filtered through leaves in scattered fragments.
Vashishtha walked ahead without speaking.
Rama and Lakshmana followed a few steps behind.
For a long while, only the sound of their footsteps on dry leaves moved through the forest.
Then Vashishtha slowed.
He raised his hand slightly.
They stopped.
Not far ahead, in a small clearing, a deer stood grazing. Its ears twitched constantly. Its body remained soft, but alert. Every few moments its head lifted, eyes scanning the forest.
Vashishtha watched the deer quietly.
After some time he spoke softly, without turning.
“Rama.”
“Yes, Gurudev.”
“What is that deer doing?”
Rama observed carefully.
“It is eating, Gurudev.”
Vashishtha remained silent.
Lakshmana spoke.
“It is also watching the forest.”
Vashishtha nodded slightly.
“Look again.”
They watched.
The deer lowered its head to graze. Yet even while eating, its ears moved in every direction. Its body seemed relaxed, but nothing around it escaped its awareness.
After a few moments the deer suddenly lifted its head.
Its ears froze.
Some distant sound had touched it.
For several breaths it stood perfectly still.
Then, sensing no danger, it returned to grazing.
Vashishtha turned to them.
“You see this animal.”
They nodded.
“It is eating. Yet it is not lost in eating.”
“It is resting. Yet it is not lost in resting.”
“It moves through the forest with its life in its ears.”
Lakshmana frowned slightly.
“In its ears, Gurudev?”
Vashishtha smiled faintly.
“In its attention.”
They watched the deer again.
Vashishtha continued.
“If the deer forgets the forest even for a moment, the tiger does not forget the deer.”
The wind moved softly through the trees.
A few dry leaves fell.
Rama spoke quietly.
“Gurudev… the deer is alert because danger may come.”
“Yes.”
Vashishtha bent down and picked up a small fallen twig.
He drew a thin line in the dust of the forest floor.
“Look carefully.”
They leaned slightly closer.
“This deer does not think about attention.”
“It lives in attention.”
Lakshmana asked,
“Is this why you brought us here, Gurudev?”
Vashishtha stood again.
“Yes.”
He began walking once more. They followed.
After some time he spoke again.
“Yesterday we spoke of attention.”
“Today you will see how easily it is stolen.”
They continued walking deeper into the forest.
Soon the path opened beside a small flowing stream.
The water moved gently over smooth stones, making a soft, continuous sound.
Vashishtha stopped.
He pointed toward the stream.
“Rama, bring me a pot of water.”
Rama nodded and walked toward the stream.
As he knelt near the water, a brilliant blue kingfisher suddenly darted across the surface of the stream and landed on a branch nearby.
Its feathers flashed in the sunlight.
Lakshmana whispered softly behind him.
“Rama, look.”
Rama turned.
For a brief moment he watched the bird.
The kingfisher tilted its head and plunged into the water, emerging with a tiny fish.
Lakshmana smiled.
But behind them, Vashishtha’s voice came calmly.
“Rama.”
Rama immediately turned back.
“Yes, Gurudev.”
“You went to bring water.”
Rama lowered his eyes slightly.
“Yes, Gurudev.”
“Did you bring water?”
Rama looked at the empty pot still in his hand.
“No, Gurudev.”
Vashishtha’s face held no displeasure.
Only stillness.
“What happened?”
Rama spoke honestly.
“My attention went to the bird.”
Vashishtha nodded.
“The bird did not steal your attention.”
“You gave it.”
They stood quietly beside the stream.
The water continued its soft movement.
Then Vashishtha walked to the edge of the water and filled the pot himself.
He placed it back into Rama’s hands.
“Again.”
Rama stepped forward once more.
This time he knelt.
The stream moved.
Birds called in the distance.
Leaves shifted above.
But Rama’s hands moved steadily.
He filled the pot and stood.
When he returned, Vashishtha looked at him carefully.
“Did the forest disappear?”
“No, Gurudev.”
“Did the sounds stop?”
“No.”
“Then what changed?”
Rama reflected for a moment.
“My attention did not leave the task.”
Vashishtha nodded slowly.
Lakshmana was watching his brother closely.
Vashishtha looked at both of them.
“Attention is like a lamp.”
“When it is steady, the world is seen clearly.”
“When it flickers, the world pulls it in many directions.”
He pointed gently toward the forest around them.
“The forest is not the disturbance.”
“The wandering of attention is the disturbance.”
They stood silently.
The wind passed through the tall trees again.
After a long pause, Lakshmana spoke.
“Gurudev…”
“Yes.”
“Can attention become as steady as the deer?”
Vashishtha looked at him with quiet warmth.
“Yes.”
Lakshmana waited.
“How?”
Vashishtha began walking again along the stream.
“By first seeing how restless it truly is.”
They followed him.
After some distance he stopped beside a large flat stone.
He sat down.
Rama and Lakshmana sat before him.
For a long time Vashishtha said nothing.
The forest breathed around them.
Finally he spoke very softly.
“Today you have seen how attention moves.”
“Tomorrow…”
He looked into Rama’s eyes.
“…you will see who it is that moves it.”
The forest had become very quiet.
