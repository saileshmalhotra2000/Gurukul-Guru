Gurukul Session 1
Topic: Mastery of Attention
Rishi Vashishta would not begin with philosophy.
He would begin with this:
“Rama, Lakshmana — learn first to hold your mind.”
Not control the world.
Control attention.
Because:
A distracted prince becomes a dangerous king.
A steady mind becomes a just ruler.
🪵 The Teaching (As Vashishta Might Say)
“Child, the mind runs like an untied calf.
If you chase it, it runs more.
If you beat it, it rebels.
If you ignore it, it wanders into fields that are not yours.
So what must you do?
Tie it gently to one post.”
The post is attention.
🧘 Today’s Gurukul Practice (20 Minutes)
This is not meditation for peace.
This is training.
Step 1 — Sit (5 minutes)
Sit without moving.
No scratching.
No adjusting.
No shifting posture.
If the body moves, the mind wins.
Let the body obey you.
Step 2 — One Object (10 minutes)
Pick one simple thing:
Your breath
 OR
The flame of a lamp
 OR
The sound of a single tanpura drone (you are a tabla master — you understand laya deeply)
Hold attention there.
The moment the mind drifts — bring it back.
No frustration.
No drama.
Just bring it back.
Like guiding a child.
Do this again and again.
Rama would have done this daily.
Step 3 — Observe the Mind (5 minutes)
After finishing, ask:
How restless was my mind?
How many times did it wander?
Did I get irritated?
Do not judge.
Just observe.
🌿 Why This Matters
Before Rama learned archery,
Before scriptures,
Before statecraft,
He learned to hold his mind steady.
Because a warrior with unstable attention misses the target.
A king with unstable attention ruins a kingdom.
And in modern times?
A distracted man ruins his own life.
The morning is quiet. A faint mist lingers near the river. Rama and Lakshmana sit before Vashishtha, their bows resting beside them.
Vashishtha looks at them, then at the open forest.
“Rama, Lakshmana… before you learn to rule others, learn to hold your own mind.”
Lakshmana straightens.
“Gurudev, should we not first learn the scriptures? Or the bow?”
Vashishtha shakes his head gently.
“A distracted prince becomes a danger to his people.”
He pauses.
“A steady mind becomes a just ruler.”
Rama listens deeply.
“So what must we do, Gurudev?”
Vashishtha points toward a young calf grazing nearby, its rope loosely tied.
“Watch.”
A sudden sound startles the calf. It runs wildly in circles, pulling against the rope.
“If you chase it,” Vashishtha says, “it runs faster.”
“If you beat it, it resists.”
“If you leave it untied, it wanders into fields that are not yours.”
Lakshmana nods.
“Yes, Gurudev.”
Vashishtha turns to them.
“So what must be done?”
Rama answers softly.
“It must be tied.”
“Yes.”
“But not harshly.”
He gestures gently.
“Firmly… and with care.”
The calf begins to settle.
“The mind is like this.”
Lakshmana smiles slightly.
“It runs just the same.”
Vashishtha nods.
“So do not chase the mind.
 Do not fight the mind.
 Do not abandon the mind.”
He looks at Rama.
“Anchor it.”
Rama asks,
“To what, Gurudev?”
Vashishtha points toward the still river.
“To one thing.”
Silence settles.
“Come,” he says.
They move closer to the riverbank.
Vashishtha sits.
“Sit.”
The brothers sit.
“Now remain as you are.”
Lakshmana adjusts slightly.
Vashishtha glances at him.
“Do not move.”
Lakshmana freezes.
“If the body wanders,” Vashishtha says,
 “the mind has already wandered.”
Rama becomes completely still.
Time passes.
A bird calls. The wind shifts. A leaf falls near Lakshmana’s hand — but he does not move.
After some time, Vashishtha speaks again.
“Now, Rama… place your attention on the breath.”
Rama closes his eyes gently.
“Lakshmana… you may listen to the river.”
Lakshmana nods.
“Hold your attention there.”
Silence deepens.
Moments pass.
Then—
Lakshmana’s brow tightens slightly.
His attention drifts.
He notices a bird.
Then a thought.
Then another.
He pulls his attention back.
Again it slips.
Again he brings it back.
Vashishtha watches quietly.
After some time, he speaks.
“When the mind wanders…”
Lakshmana answers softly, eyes still closed,
“…bring it back.”
“How?”
“Without anger.”
Rama adds quietly,
“Without frustration.”
Vashishtha nods.
“Yes.”
“Like guiding a child… not punishing it.”
More silence.
The river continues its steady sound.
After a long while, Vashishtha says,
“Open your eyes.”
The brothers open them slowly.
Lakshmana exhales.
“My mind ran everywhere, Gurudev.”
Vashishtha smiles faintly.
“Good.”
Rama remains quiet.
“And you?” Vashishtha asks.
“It wandered less,” Rama replies softly,
 “but I saw how easily it moves.”
Vashishtha nods.
“That is the beginning of mastery.”
He looks at both of them.
“Do not judge the mind.”
“Observe it.”
Lakshmana asks,
“Gurudev… why is this so important?”
Vashishtha picks up a small stone and tosses it toward a distant tree.
It misses.
“A warrior whose attention wavers… misses the target.”
He looks at Rama.
“A king whose attention wavers… harms his people.”
The river flows quietly beside them.
“And a man whose attention wavers…”
He lets the words settle.
“…loses himself.”
Silence fills the forest.
Vashishtha stands.
“Each day, you will train like this.”
Rama bows his head.
Lakshmana nods firmly.
The morning sun rises higher.
And beside the flowing river, two princes begin learning the first discipline —
 not of the bow…
 but of the mind.

Friday, 13 March 2026
The forest is quiet after dawn. A soft breeze moves through the banyan leaves. The river flows steadily, as if repeating the same lesson again and again.
Lakshmana speaks, a little impatient.
“Gurudev… when my mind wanders, I try to bring it back… but it feels like I am forcing it.”
Rama nods.
“Yes. And sometimes the more I try, the more it slips away.”
Vashishtha looks at them with gentle attention.
“Come,” he says.
He rises and walks a short distance along the riverbank. A small child from the ashram is playing nearby, chasing a butterfly.
The child runs too far toward the water’s edge.
Vashishtha calls softly,
“Come back.”
The child pauses, looks, and returns slowly.
Vashishtha turns to the brothers.
“Did I pull him?”
“No,” Lakshmana says.
“Did I shout in anger?”
“No.”
“Did I ignore him?”
“No.”
Rama watches the child return.
“You called him… and he came.”
Vashishtha nods.
“That is how you guide the mind.”
Lakshmana frowns slightly.
“But Gurudev… the mind does not always listen.”
Vashishtha smiles faintly.
“Does the child always listen the first time?”
Lakshmana smiles.
“No.”
“What do you do then?”
“I call again.”
“Yes.”
The sage looks toward the flowing river.
“When the mind wanders, do not strike it.”
He gestures gently.
“Do not say, ‘Why are you like this?’”
Lakshmana lowers his eyes slightly.
“Do not say, ‘I must control you.’”
Rama listens deeply.
“Simply notice…”
He pauses.
“…and call it back.”
Lakshmana asks,
“With what words, Gurudev?”
Vashishtha shakes his head.
“Not with words.”
He places his hand lightly on his chest.
“With attention itself.”
Silence settles.
“When you realize the mind has wandered…”
He looks at Rama.
“…that very realization is already the beginning of returning.”
Rama’s breath slows.
“So the moment of noticing is enough?”
“Yes.”
“What follows must be gentle.”
Lakshmana tilts his head.
“But sometimes I feel irritation.”
Vashishtha nods.
“That irritation is also seen.”
He points toward the river.
“Do not fight even that.”
Lakshmana exhales slowly.
“So everything is brought back… with the same gentleness.”
“Yes.”
Vashishtha bends and picks up a leaf floating near the edge of the water.
He places it back into the current.
“See?”
He lets it drift.
“I do not push it harshly.”
“I place it back into the flow.”
Rama watches the leaf being carried away.
“So bringing the mind back is not an act of force…”
“…but an act of remembering,” he says softly.
Vashishtha’s eyes warm.
“Yes.”
“The mind wanders.”
“That is its nature.”
“But the one who remembers…”
He looks at both of them.
“…need not struggle.”
The river continues its quiet journey.
And in that gentle movement, the princes begin to understand —
 that mastery of the mind does not come from tightening the fist…
 but from learning how to open the hand. 🌿
