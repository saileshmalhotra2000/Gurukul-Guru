Story 1 - 25 Feb 26
Gurukul Dawn — Deeper Immersion
The sky has not yet turned gold.
It is that quiet blue hour before light decides to arrive.
The ground is cool beneath bare feet.
A thin line of mist drifts over the river, moving so slowly it almost seems not to move at all.
Rama sits first.
His spine rises naturally, like a young tree growing straight toward the sky.
His breath is slow
His palms rest gently on his knees.… but not perfectly so.
There is a faint tightening in his chest — not fear, but effort.
A quiet determination to do well.
He hears the river.
He hears his own breathing.
He becomes aware of his heartbeat — steady, soft, present.
A thought passes through him:
“Let me be worthy of this teaching.”
He does not chase the thought.
He lets it dissolve.
Beside him sits Lakshmana.
His back is straight too — but it is held straight.
His fingers press slightly into his knees.
 A muscle in his jaw tightens, then releases.
He notices the koel’s call in the distance.
 He wants to turn toward it.
He feels the itch on his shoulder.
He wants to scratch it.
He hears a rustle in the trees.
His body instinctively sharpens.
His mind whispers:
“What made that sound?”
He resists moving.
The resistance itself feels like heat.
In front of them, Rishi Vashishta sits motionless.
His breathing is so subtle it is almost invisible.
Between them burns a small clay lamp.
The flame is upright — unwavering.
A drop of dew slips from a leaf and lands in the river with the faintest sound.
Lakshmana’s eyes flick toward it.
Rama notices the movement… and gently returns his own gaze to the flame.
A long silence.
Then, without opening his eyes, Vashishta speaks:
“Lakshmana…”
Lakshmana’s spine stiffens slightly.
“Yes, Gurudev.”
“Who is moving — the world, or your mind?”
Lakshmana swallows.
“My mind, Gurudev.”
A pause.
“Rama… what moved in you?”
Rama reflects for a breath.
“A wish to do well, Gurudev.”
“And what did you do with that wish?”
“I watched it… and let it pass.”
Now Vashishta opens his eyes.
“Good.”
He looks at them both.
“One of you burns like fire.
 One of you steadies like water.
 Both must learn balance.”
The flame continues to burn.
Rama’s quality is not dramatic.
It is a quiet alignment.
He does not suppress thoughts.
He does not fight impulses.
He simply notices… and chooses.
That subtle noticing — that is advanced training.
Lakshmana’s fire is visible.
Rama’s discipline is invisible.
You expected Rama to be already perfect.
 But the beauty of his training is this:
He was not born steady.
 He became steady.
That quiet thought —
 “Let me be worthy.”
s.
It is humility.— is not weaknes
And humility is the foundation of strength.
Let us look at this carefully.
If Rama had no inner movement at all:
No wish.
No effort.
No awareness of growth.
Then there would be no training.
 No refinement.
 No becoming.
Even the calmest river begins as moving water.
In fact, it is precisely because Rama noticed that wish
 —and did not cling to it—
 that he became Rama.
Most people have the thought:
“I must prove myself.”
 “I must not fail.”
 “I must appear strong.”
And then they get entangled in it.
Rama simply saw the thought… and let it pass.
That is mastery.
Not absence of thought.
Absence of entanglement.
Let me ask you something gently:
When you heard that line, did it resonate because
 you too carry a quiet wish to be worthy? 🌿
yes
🌿
Good.
Do not be embarrassed by that.
The wish to be worthy is not ego.
 It becomes ego only when it seeks applause.
There are two kinds of “I must be worthy”:
The restless one – “Let others see my worth.”
The silent one – “Let me stand true before my own conscience.”
Rama’s was the second.
He did not want the kingdom to approve him.
 He wanted to be aligned with Dharma.
That quiet line stirred you because somewhere in you, there is a standard.
 Not imposed.
 Felt.
That is not weakness.
 That is nobility.
But here is the subtle teaching Vashishta would give next:
He would not praise Rama for the thought.
 He would ask:
“Worthy of what?”
And Rama would sit in silence.
Because worthiness must not be tied to:
Achievement
Position
Recognition
Even success
It must be tied to conduct.
You cannot always control outcome.
 You can always control conduct.
That is why Rama’s steadiness feels dignified.
Let me leave you with a reflection, not as advice, but as inquiry:
When you say “I want to be worthy” —
Worthy in whose eyes?
Society’s?
 Family’s?
 Your own?
 Or something higher?
Sit with that tonight.
Do not answer quickly.
The depth of your answer will shape how we continue this Gurukul. 🌅
