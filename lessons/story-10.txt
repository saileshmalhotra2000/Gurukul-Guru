Friday, 6 March 2026
Morning light spreads slowly through the forest clearing. The sound of the river is deeper today, swollen from distant mountain rains. Birds call from the tall sal trees, their voices echoing softly.
Rama and Lakshmana sit before Vashishtha on the cool earth.
Rama’s brow is still thoughtful.
“Gurudev,” he says quietly, “yesterday you spoke of examining intention, effect, and the state of the mind.”
Vashishtha nods but does not yet speak.
Lakshmana breaks a small twig beside him, turning it between his fingers.
“But sometimes,” Lakshmana says, “two paths both appear right.”
Vashishtha lifts his gaze toward the forest path leading east.
“Come,” he says.
The three rise and walk a short distance until the narrow path divides into two trails winding between the trees.
They stop where the ground forks.
Vashishtha points.
“Rama, if a traveler stands here and does not know which road leads to the village, what must he do?”
Rama studies the two paths.
“He must observe.”
“What will he observe?”
“The signs.”
“Which signs?”
Rama kneels and touches the soil.
“Footprints.”
Lakshmana crouches beside him.
“This path has deeper tracks,” he says. “Many people have walked here.”
Vashishtha nods slightly.
“And this one?”
Lakshmana examines the other trail.
“It is narrow… and overgrown.”
The wind stirs the leaves overhead.
Vashishtha looks at them both.
“When Dharma is unclear, the wise man searches for signs.”
Rama rises.
“What signs, Gurudev?”
Vashishtha gestures toward the ground.
“First, the signs left by those who walked before.”
Rama understands.
“The elders.”
“Yes.”
“The sages.”
“Yes.”
“The laws that have protected people through time.”
Lakshmana’s eyes brighten.
“So we follow their footprints.”
Vashishtha smiles faintly.
“Until we can see clearly ourselves.”
He steps slowly onto the wider path but does not walk further.
“Second,” he continues, “observe where the path leads.”
Rama follows his gaze.
In the distance, faint smoke curls above the trees — the cooking fires of the nearby village.
“The right path,” Rama says softly, “moves toward life.”
“Yes.”
Vashishtha turns to them.
“A path that leads toward harmony, protection, and order is rarely wrong.”
The forest breeze cools the morning air.
Lakshmana crosses his arms.
“But Gurudev… sometimes the crowd walks the wrong road.”
Vashishtha’s eyes glimmer with approval.
“Good.”
He turns and steps off the wide path into the quiet one.
Leaves crunch softly beneath his feet.
“Which is why the third sign is the most important.”
Rama and Lakshmana follow.
The forest here is quieter, deeper.
Vashishtha stops beside a still pond hidden among the trees.
The water is perfectly calm.
“Look.”
Both brothers gaze at their reflections.
“When the mind becomes still,” Vashishtha says gently, “truth begins to reveal itself.”
Rama studies the reflection of the sky on the water.
“Even when the road is empty.”
“Yes.”
Lakshmana asks quietly,
“And if the mind refuses to become still?”
Vashishtha bends and picks up a small stone.
He tosses it into the pond.
Ripples spread across the surface.
“Then wait.”
The ripples slowly fade.
The water clears again.
“Right action rarely demands haste.”
Rama watches the last circle disappear.
“So Dharma is discovered by patience.”
Vashishtha looks at him warmly.
“Patience… humility… and sincere observation.”
The forest is now fully awake. Sunlight slips between the tall trunks and touches the surface of the pond.
Vashishtha begins walking back toward the clearing.
“One who rushes to act often serves the ego.”
Rama and Lakshmana follow behind him.
“But one who learns to see clearly,” Vashishtha says softly, “begins to serve Dharma itself.”
The river's steady sound greets them again as they return.
And the path through the forest no longer seems confusing.
The sun has not yet risen. The forest is wrapped in a cool blue light. A faint breeze moves across the river, and the surface trembles softly.
Rama sits with the question still alive in him.
“Gurudev… yesterday you spoke of following the footprints of those who walked before us — the laws that have protected people through time. What are these laws?”
Vashishtha listens quietly.
He bends and draws a wide circle in the sand.
“Rama, tell me… what allows a forest to live for centuries?”
Rama looks around him.
“The balance of life, Gurudev.”
“Explain.”
“The trees grow. The animals eat. The rivers flow. Nothing takes more than the forest can give.”
Vashishtha nods slowly.
“These are the laws.”
Lakshmana looks puzzled.
“Laws of the forest?”
“Yes.”
He points toward the river.
“Water flows downward.
 Fire burns upward.
 Seeds grow toward light.”
He pauses.
“No king created these laws.
 Yet if they are broken, life suffers.”
The river murmurs beside them.
“In the same way,” Vashishtha continues,
 “human societies discovered certain principles that preserve harmony.”
He draws four small marks in the sand.
“Truthfulness.”
He taps the first mark.
“If people abandon truth, trust collapses.”
He taps the second.
“Responsibility.”
“If people abandon responsibility, chaos spreads.”
He taps the third.
“Respect for life.”
“If people abandon it, fear fills the world.”
He taps the fourth.
“Self-restraint.”
“If desire rules without restraint, suffering multiplies.”
Rama listens intently.
“These principles,” Vashishtha says quietly,
 “were not invented by one man.”
“They were discovered through centuries of living.”
Lakshmana asks,
“Discovered?”
“Yes.”
“When a society honored truth, it flourished.”
“When it abandoned truth, it decayed.”
He smooths the sand slightly.
“Over time, wise people observed these patterns.”
“And they preserved them.”
Rama speaks softly.
“So Dharma is not merely a rule.”
“No.”
“It is the intelligence that keeps life in balance.”
The first rays of sunlight begin to touch the treetops.
“When you are uncertain,” Vashishtha says gently,
 “look to these enduring principles.”
He gestures to the forest.
“They are like the deep roots of a tree.”
“Storms may shake the branches,
 but the roots hold the earth together.”
Lakshmana looks thoughtful.
“And if a situation is new… and the old rules do not clearly apply?”
Vashishtha smiles faintly.
“Then return to the roots.”
He taps the four marks again.
“Truth.
 Responsibility.
 Respect for life.
 Restraint.”
“If your action protects these,
 you are rarely far from Dharma.”
The forest brightens as the sun lifts higher.
Birds cross the river in wide arcs of flight.
And the ancient laws continue quietly —
 not written on stone alone,
 but living in the rhythm of the world itself.
