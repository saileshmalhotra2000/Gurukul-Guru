Dawn arrives without announcement.
The forest exhales night. Ash-grey smoke rises from the dying embers of the fire. Birds test their voices, then commit.
Rama and Lakshmana approach quietly.
Vashishtha is seated as before, eyes half‑closed, spine unmoving. He opens his eyes as if he had been listening to their footsteps long before they made them.
“Sit,” he says.
They sit.
For a long while, nothing else happens.
Lakshmana shifts slightly. Rama remains still.
Vashishtha’s gaze rests on Rama.
“Last night,” he says, “what guarded your thoughts?”
Rama answers honestly.
 “The teaching, Gurudev. And the watching of myself.”
“And who was watching?”
Rama pauses.
 “The ‘I’… but more quietly than before.”
Vashishtha nods.
“When the ‘I’ learns it is being watched,” he says, “it lowers its voice.”
He turns to Lakshmana.
“And you?”
Lakshmana replies,
 “I noticed how quickly I wished to act—to protect, to correct. I saw pride even in readiness.”
Vashishtha’s eyes soften.
“Good. A sword that knows its own sharpness cuts less foolishly.”
A breeze moves through the sal trees.
Vashishtha picks up a handful of sand and lets it fall slowly back to the earth.
“Today,” he says, “you will learn the difference between holding the ego and being held by it.”
Rama’s attention sharpens.
“When hunger comes,” Vashishtha continues, “you eat. Is hunger your enemy?”
“No, Gurudev.”
“When sleep comes, you rest. Is sleep an obstacle?”
“No.”
“Yet if hunger commands you, you steal.
 If sleep commands you, you abandon duty.”
He lets the last grains of sand fall.
“The ego is like hunger and sleep.
 It signals. It gathers. It motivates.”
Lakshmana speaks.
 “But Gurudev, sometimes it lies.”
“Yes,” Vashishtha replies.
 “And so does the tongue. Do you cut it out?”
Lakshmana smiles faintly.
 “No.”
“You train it.”
Silence settles again.
Vashishtha looks toward the river.
“Rama,” he says, “one day you will be praised beyond measure. Songs will be sung. Men will kneel.”
Rama remains still.
“If you have made an enemy of the ego,” Vashishtha continues,
 “you will either believe the songs…
 or pretend not to hear them while secretly feeding on them.”
Lakshmana’s brow furrows.
“But if you have seen the ego clearly,” Vashishtha says,
 “you will use it to stand, to speak, to decide—
 and then set it aside.”
He turns back to them.
“A king must say ‘I command.’
 A warrior must say ‘I will strike.’
 A protector must say ‘I will not allow this.’”
His voice is calm, firm.
“These are not bondage.
 Bondage begins when the voice adds:
 ‘Because I am greater.’”
Rama bows his head slightly.
Vashishtha notices.
“Do not bow to me for this,” he says gently.
 “Bow to the seeing.”
The forest listens.
“Today’s silence,” Vashishtha adds, “will be longer than yesterday’s.”
He rises.
“You will walk separately.
 Speak to no one.
 Act when needed.
 Notice when ‘I’ arises—
 and whether it serves the moment or demands to be remembered.”
He pauses, then says quietly:
“When the ego is an instrument,
 action is clean.
 When the ego is master,
 even virtue leaves residue.”
He turns and walks toward the deeper forest.
Rama and Lakshmana remain seated for a moment longer.
Then they rise—
 not as students seeking perfection,
 but as young warriors learning how to carry their own strength without letting it carry them.
The forest closes behind them.

You had said “The ego is like hunger and sleep.  It signals. It gathers. It motivates.” Can you please explain more
Sit for a moment, my friend, and let us look at this quietly.
When I said “the ego is like hunger and sleep”, I did not mean it as an enemy.
 In the ancient teachings, the ego (ahamkara) is not considered evil. It is a functional force of nature, just like hunger, thirst, or the need for sleep.
Let us explore this gently.
1. Ego as a Signal
When the body is hungry, a signal appears:
 “I need food.”
When the body is tired, another signal appears:
 “I need rest.”
Similarly, the ego produces signals like:
“I want to achieve something.”
“I want respect.”
“I want to protect myself.”
“I want recognition.”
These are movement signals in the mind.
Without them, a person might not act at all.
 Even a child learning to walk has a small ego impulse:
 “I will try again.”
So the ego alerts the system to act, just as hunger alerts the body to eat.
2. Ego as a Gatherer
Hunger gathers food.
Sleep gathers energy.
Ego gathers identity.
It collects things like:
my name
my abilities
my story
my achievements
my beliefs
It builds the feeling of “I am this person.”
This gathering is useful.
 Otherwise daily life would be impossible.
Imagine waking up and not remembering:
your skills
your responsibilities
your role in the world
The ego organizes the personality so life can function.
3. Ego as Motivation
Hunger makes the body move toward food.
Sleep makes the body move toward rest.
Ego makes the mind move toward goals.
It pushes things like:
learning a skill
protecting family
building something meaningful
mastering an art
Even your 20 years of teaching tabla likely required this force at times:
“Let me improve.”
 “Let me practice.”
 “Let me teach well.”
That motivating spark often comes from the ego.
Without it, effort becomes very weak.
4. Where the Problem Begins
Hunger is healthy.
But constant overeating becomes suffering.
Sleep is healthy.
But sleeping all day becomes dullness.
In the same way:
Ego is natural.
But constant identification with it becomes bondage.
For example:
Instead of
 “Respect is pleasant”
the ego turns it into
 “I must always be respected.”
Instead of
 “I played well today”
it becomes
 “I am my performance.”
This is where suffering grows.
5. The Role of Awareness
The sages did not try to destroy the ego.
They simply placed it in its proper seat.
Just as you notice hunger without becoming hunger,
 you can notice ego impulses without becoming them.
Example:
A thought appears:
“I want appreciation.”
Instead of fighting it, simply see:
“Ah… the ego is asking for food.”
Just seeing it loosens the grip.
6. The Deeper Discovery
When awareness becomes steady, something surprising is noticed.
Hunger appears.
 Sleep appears.
 Ego impulses appear.
But something is watching all of them.
That silent watcher is what the sages called:
sakshi (the witness)
chaitanya (pure awareness)
It is not hungry.
 It is not proud.
 It simply knows the movements of life.
So the teaching is not:
“Destroy the ego.”
The teaching is:
“Let the ego be a servant, not the king.”
Like a horse pulling a chariot.
Very useful.
 Very powerful.
But the rider must remain awake.
