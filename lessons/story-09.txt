Thursday, 5 March 2026
The dawn is cool. A thin mist clings to the river. The forest is not yet fully awake.
Rama’s voice is quiet.
“Gurudev… how does ego learn to rest in Dharma?”
Vashishtha does not answer at once.
He watches a young deer step cautiously to the river’s edge.
“Rama,” he says softly, “when a horse is untamed, what must be done?”
Lakshmana answers,
 “It must be trained, Gurudev.”
“How?”
“With firmness… and guidance.”
Vashishtha nods.
“The ego is like that horse.”
He turns to Rama.
“If left alone, it runs toward pleasure and away from discomfort.”
He draws two arrows in the sand — one toward a flower, one away from a thorn.
“But Dharma is not chosen by pleasure or pain.”
Silence rests between them.
“First,” Vashishtha continues,
 “you must know your Dharma.”
Rama listens intently.
“If you do not know what is right, ego will rest wherever it is fed.”
Lakshmana frowns slightly.
“And how do we know?”
Vashishtha’s gaze sharpens.
“Ask:
 Does this action protect truth?
 Does it protect life?
 Does it protect responsibility?”
He pauses.
“If it protects only image… it is not Dharma.”
The deer lowers its head to drink.
“Second,” he says,
 “act according to that understanding — even when praise does not follow.”
Rama’s breath deepens.
“When ego sees that acting in alignment brings inner steadiness… it begins to prefer that food.”
Lakshmana tilts his head.
“So ego changes its taste?”
“Yes.”
He gestures toward the river.
“If a child eats only sweets, he craves sweets.”
He picks up a wild berry from the ground.
“If he learns the taste of grain and fruit, his body grows strong.”
He looks at Rama.
“Each time you act rightly without seeking recognition, ego weakens its addiction to praise.”
Silence expands.
“Third,” Vashishtha says softly,
 “reflect each evening.”
Lakshmana glances up.
“Not with judgment.”
He taps his chest lightly.
“With honesty.”
“Where did I act for image?”
 “Where did I act for truth?”
Rama lowers his gaze.
“This quiet seeing trains ego to rest where there is peace.”
The sun begins to rise higher.
“Ego rests in Dharma,” Vashishtha concludes,
 “when it discovers that inner steadiness is sweeter than applause.”
He looks at both boys.
“The horse runs wildly at first.”
A faint smile appears.
“But when it feels the strength of steady ground beneath it…
 it begins to prefer the path.”
The mist thins.
The river continues flowing — not toward noise, not away from shadow —
 but simply along its course.
And the servant within slowly learns
 where to stand.
Thursday, 5 March 2026
The dawn arrives quietly. The river carries a pale band of light. A faint fog rests over the water.
Rama sits very still.
After some time he speaks.
“Gurudev… yesterday you said that when ego rests in Dharma it asks only one question — ‘Was the action right?’”
He lifts his eyes.
“How do we know what is right?”
Vashishtha does not answer immediately.
He reaches down and picks up a bowl of clear water from beside him.
“Rama, look into this.”
Rama leans forward and sees his reflection.
“What do you see?”
“My face, Gurudev.”
Vashishtha stirs the water gently with his finger. The reflection trembles.
“And now?”
“It is unclear.”
Lakshmana watches closely.
“When the water is disturbed,” Vashishtha says quietly,
“even a true face appears distorted.”
He sets the bowl down.
“The first step in knowing what is right is a quiet mind.”
Rama nods slowly.
“When the mind is agitated by anger, fear, pride, or desire…”
He moves the water again.
“…judgment becomes cloudy.”
The surface trembles.
Lakshmana asks,
“So we must first become calm?”
“Yes.”
He waits until the water becomes still again.
“Only then can the reflection be trusted.”
Silence settles between them.
Vashishtha continues.
“Second — ask whether the action protects Dharma.”
Lakshmana tilts his head.
“And Dharma means…?”
Vashishtha answers slowly.
“Truth.
Responsibility.
Protection of life.
Fairness.”
He looks at Rama.
“If an action protects only your pride, it is not Dharma.”
The river moves softly beside them.
“Third,” he says,
“look at the result within.”
Rama listens closely.
“When you imagine the action…”
He places his palm on his chest.
“…does the heart feel contracted or open?”
Lakshmana presses his own chest unconsciously.
“Right action often feels steady inside — even if it is difficult.”
He pauses.
“Wrong action often feels uneasy — even if it promises comfort.”
A bird calls sharply from the trees.
Rama asks quietly,
“And if the mind still doubts?”
Vashishtha smiles faintly.
“Then wait.”
Lakshmana looks surprised.
“Wait?”
“Yes.”
“Dharma rarely demands haste.”
He gestures toward the river.
“The current knows its direction without rushing.”
The fog begins to lift.
“When the mind is quiet…
when the action protects truth…
when the heart feels steady…”
He looks at both boys.
“Then you are close to what is right.”
Rama bows his head slightly.
Lakshmana’s posture grows calm.
“And remember,” Vashishtha says softly,
“Even the wisest may sometimes err.”
The boys look up.
“But the one who sincerely seeks Dharma…”
He taps the ground gently with his staff.
“…corrects his path quickly.”
The river flows on —
reflecting sky, branch, and bird alike —
but only clearly when its surface is still.
